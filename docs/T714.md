<h1 id="calcium-administration">Calcium Administration</h1>
<h2 id="all">ALL</h2>
<p><strong>I. Inclusion Criteria</strong></p>
<p>A. Patient’s age is 16 years or older. -AND-</p>
<p>B. Cardiac arrest -AND/OR- suspected severe hyperkalemia -AND-</p>
<h2 id="medic">Medic</h2>
<p>C. EKG Findings indicate normal sinus rhythm, sinus tachycardia or atrial fibrillation with controlled ventricular response. If other rhythm is present, then proceed to the appropriate arrhythmia protocol.</p>
<h2 id="emt">EMT</h2>
<p><strong>II. Protocol</strong></p>
<p>A. Consider ALS if required.</p>
<p>B. Consider advanced airway management if required.</p>
<p>C. IV calcium contraindications:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Hypercalcemia</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Digoxin toxicity</p>
</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="medic-1">Medic</h2>
<p>D. Establish IV access in a large vein. IO access may be considered if IV access is not feasible.</p>
<p>E. Obtain a diagnostic EKG.</p>
<p>F. Administer calcium as per instructions below. It is very important to know which type(s) of calcium your agency may carry. Preference is for calcium chloride in cardiac arrest.</p>
<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<p>A. Different salt forms of calcium exist. Pay close attention to salt form when administering IV calcium.</p>
<p>B. 1g calcium chloride = 3g calcium gluconate</p>
<p>C. Calcium chloride:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>3 times the ionized calcium content as calcium gluconate.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Preferred in emergent situations (i.e., arrest) but has a higher potential for infusion site reactions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Avoid extravasation. May dilute in NS or D5W to prevent skin necrosis if extravasation occurs. If extravasation occurs, immediately discontinue the IV site. Notify the receiving facility at care handoff of the extravasation as skin monitoring is needed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If given before or after sodium bicarbonate, flush line with 20 mL of NS between medications (as calcium and bicarbonate may precipitate)</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>D. Dosing and administration:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Cardiac arrest - PEA or asystole: administer IV calcium chloride 20mg/kg (max 1g) IVP. May repeat if necessary. See protocol C301.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Severe hyperkalemia: administer IV calcium chloride 500-1000 mg diluted in 50-100 mL of NS over 2-5 minutes. May repeat after 5 minutes if EKG changes persist or recur. See protocol M418.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Crush injuries: administer IV calcium chloride 500-1000 mg diluted in 50-100 mL of NS over 2-5 minutes. See protocol S501.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>E. Calcium gluconate:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>1/3 the ionized calcium content as calcium chloride. Lower potential for infusion site reactions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Dosing and administration:</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>a. Cardiac arrest - PEA or asystole: administer IV calcium gluconate 3 g (30mL of calcium gluconate 100mg/mL) IVP. See protocol C301. Consider IV calcium chloride first line if available.</p>
<p>b. Hyperkalemia-associated ECG changes: administer IV calcium gluconate 2g IVP. May repeat after 5 min if ECG changes persist or recur.</p>
<p>c. Crush injuries: administer IV calcium gluconate 2g IVP.</p>
